Learning Section 1

NHS Policy & The 35 Conditions

Understanding the 2018 NHS England guidance and when prescribing exceptions apply.

🏛 NHS 2018 Guidance — Background

💷 Annual Savings

Estimated £569 million/year from a "self-care first" approach. Reduces GP and pharmacy burden for self-limiting conditions.

⏱ Self-Limiting

Most minor ailments resolve within 7–14 days with appropriate self-care. NHS prescribing provides no clinical added value.

📋 The Guidance

NHS England issued guidance (March 2018) listing 35 conditions where OTC items should NOT routinely be prescribed.

⚠️ Not Absolute

The guidance does NOT prevent prescribing — it requires clinical judgement and documented justification when exceptions apply.

✅ When Prescribing IS Appropriate — Key Exceptions

#Exception CategoryExample
1Long-Term Condition — minor ailment is ongoing consequence of chronic disease or its treatmentOpioid-induced constipation in chronic pain patient
2Multiple Conditions — patient has multiple conditions and it would be inappropriate to treat in isolationSkin dryness secondary to eczema already under care

🔑 Exam Key Point

The long-term condition exception is the most commonly tested. When a minor ailment is a direct, ongoing consequence of a chronic disease or its treatment, NHS prescribing is justified — requires documented clinical reasoning.

Learning Section 2

UK Medicine Classification — Three-Tier System

GSL, P, and POM: what they mean clinically and why reclassification matters.

📦 The Three Classification Tiers

ClassFull NameAccessPharmacist RequiredKey Examples
GSLGeneral Sales ListAny retail outletNoParacetamol ×16, Ibuprofen ×16, Simple linctus
PPharmacy MedicinePharmacy only, pharmacist presentYes — must be available for consultationOmeprazole 10mg, Cetirizine 10mg, BPO 5%/10%, Loperamide, Chloramphenicol eye drops
POMPrescription-Only MedicineValid prescription requiredDispenses prescriptionOmeprazole 20–40mg, Fluticasone nasal spray (now P), Antibiotics, Codeine (≥12.8mg)

🔄 Reclassification (POM → P) — Regulatory Rationale

Minimum Effective Dose

OTC reclassification uses the lowest effective dose for the narrowest self-care indication. Higher doses require diagnosis.

Duration Restriction

OTC time limits (e.g., 2 weeks for PPIs) prevent masking serious pathology requiring clinical investigation.

MHRA Safety Design

Dose and duration restrictions are regulatory safety design — not commercial or pharmacokinetic decisions.

Pack Size Limits

GSL/P pack sizes are capped. Smaller packs reduce risk of unintentional overdose (e.g., paracetamol GSL max ×16 tablets).

🔬 Key Example — Omeprazole

OTC: 10mg daily × max 2 weeks — for symptomatic short-term GERD relief only
POM: 20–40mg — for erosive oesophagitis, H. pylori triple therapy, Barrett's oesophagus, Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (all require confirmed diagnosis + prescriber oversight)
The restriction is a regulatory safety boundary, not a commercial differential.

Learning Section 3

Clinical Assessment Frameworks

SOAP, SCHOLAR-MAC, and PQRST — how they integrate in OTC consultations.

🗂 SOAP — Universal Documentation Structure

LetterComponentWhat it Captures
SSubjectiveWhat the patient tells you — chief complaint, symptoms, history. Embed SCHOLAR-MAC here for OTC.
OObjectiveObservable/measurable data — vital signs, physical examination, test results.
AAssessmentClinical impression, working diagnosis, differential diagnoses.
PPlanTreatment, referral, follow-up, patient education.

🔍 SCHOLAR-MAC — OTC Triage Framework

LetterElementKey Questions
SSymptomsWhat is the main complaint?
CCharacteristicsQuality, severity — sharp, dull, burning; pattern over time
HHistoryPast episodes, what has been tried; chronicity
OOnsetWhen did it start? Sudden or gradual?
LLocationWhere? Does it radiate? — embed PQRST here for pain
AAggravating/AlleviatingWhat makes it worse or better?
RRemissionDoes it come and go? How long does it last?
MMedications⚠️ ALL current medications — NEVER conditional
AAllergies⚠️ Drug & food allergies — NEVER conditional
CConditions⚠️ Medical history — NEVER conditional
⚠️ Critical Rule

The MAC component (Medications, Allergies, Conditions) is the mandatory safety screen — NEVER conditional on patient disclosure. Always ask, even if the patient appears to volunteer the information unprompted.

🔬 PQRST — Pain Characterisation (embedded within SCHOLAR-MAC)

LetterElementExamples
PProvocation / PalliationWhat brings it on? What makes it better/worse?
QQualitySharp, dull, burning, cramping, throbbing, pressure?
RRadiationDoes it spread? Where? (jaw pain = cardiac; right shoulder = gallbladder)
SSeverity0–10 pain scale; functional impairment
TTimingConstant vs intermittent; duration; pattern
🔑 Framework Integration — Exam Favourite

Optimal sequence: SOAP (overarching structure) → SCHOLAR-MAC within Subjective (OTC triage) → PQRST within SCHOLAR-MAC Characteristics/Location for granular pain assessment. PQRST is NOT restricted to ward settings — it applies wherever a dominant symptom needs deep characterisation.

Learning Section 4

Key Conditions — High-Yield Clinical Points

Evidence-based management of conditions most heavily tested in the examination.

☀️ Sunburn & Photodermatoses

TopicKey Guidance
AnalgesiaParacetamol or ibuprofen for pain. In pregnancy → paracetamol only (NSAIDs CI from 2nd trimester — premature ductus arteriosus closure)
NHS ACBS SunscreenPrescribed for: PLE, xeroderma pigmentosum, porphyria. Spec: SPF 30+ broad-spectrum UVA+UVB. Year-round (not seasonal)
Red Flags → ReferBlistering >20% BSA; infection signs (pus, fever); systemic symptoms; sunburn in infants; burns to face/hands/genitalia
⚠️ Common Misconception

ACBS sunscreen prescribing for PLE is NOT seasonal. UV exposure occurs year-round (cloudy days, car windows). Year-round daily use is required.

🫧 Mild Acne — Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO)

TopicEvidence-Based Guidance
BPO Dosing StrategyStart 2.5% once daily → escalate to 5% after 2–4 weeks if tolerated. 2.5% = 10% antibacterially. Lower concentration = significantly less irritation
MechanismsAntibacterial (C. acnes), comedolytic (mild keratolytic), anti-inflammatory. Effective for BOTH comedonal AND inflammatory acne
Red Flags → ReferNodules/cysts/scarring; no response after 12 weeks OTC; psychological distress; sudden onset in adults (hormonal cause)

💊 Laxatives — Pharmacological Mechanisms

ClassMechanismExamplesKey Notes
Bulk-formingIncrease stool bulk via water absorptionIspaghula husk (Fybogel)Requires adequate fluid & peristalsis. Avoid in opioid-induced constipation
OsmoticDraw water into bowel lumenLactulose, Macrogol (PEG)Macrogol: non-fermented (no gas), renally safe, superior in elderly/opioid-induced constipation
StimulantStimulate enteric nerves → peristalsisBisacodyl, SennaMax ~1 week — long-term use risks colonic atony ("cathartic colon")
Stool softenerReduces surface tension of stoolDocusateLimited efficacy alone; useful adjunct
⭐ High-Yield Macrogol vs Lactulose

Lactulose is fermented by colonic bacteria → gas, bloating (problematic in elderly). Macrogol is non-fermented, not systemically absorbed, not renally cleared → preferred in opioid-induced constipation, elderly, renal impairment.

🤧 Allergic Rhinitis — Treatment Steps

StepPresentationTreatment
Step 1Mild intermittent symptomsOral antihistamine (loratadine 10mg or cetirizine 10mg OD)
Step 2Moderate–severe / perennialIntranasal corticosteroid (fluticasone propionate 50mcg per nostril BD)
Step 3Ocular symptomsAdd antihistamine eye drops (ketotifen, sodium cromoglicate)
⏱ Onset of Intranasal Corticosteroids

Onset: 2–3 days. Full effect: 2–4 weeks. Must be used REGULARLY (not PRN). Most common cause of apparent "failure" is PRN use and incorrect technique. Check technique: aim away from nasal septum.

💊 PPI Therapy — Critical Interactions

IssueDetail
CRITICAL Omeprazole + ClopidogrelOmeprazole is a potent CYP2C19 inhibitor. Clopidogrel is a prodrug requiring CYP2C19 for activation. Inhibition → reduced antiplatelet effect → coronary stent thrombosis risk. Prefer pantoprazole or rabeprazole (lower CYP2C19 inhibition)
OTC Duration LimitOTC PPIs: max 2 weeks. Beyond 2 weeks without medical review → risk of masking Barrett's, peptic ulcer, malignancy
Long-term RisksHypomagnesaemia (chronic use >1 year), C. difficile risk, vitamin B12 malabsorption

💊 Paracetamol — Hidden Accumulation & Alcohol Risk

🚨 Critical Safety Points

Hidden accumulation: Many products contain paracetamol (co-codamol, Night Nurse, cold & flu remedies). Always counsel on ALL sources.

Chronic alcohol use: CYP2E1 induction → more hepatotoxic NAPQI metabolite produced. Glutathione depletion → less NAPQI detoxified. Safe maximum reduced to 2g/24h (or avoid entirely).

Night Nurse contains promethazine (sedating antihistamine) — additive CNS depression with benzodiazepines/opioids.

Topic: NHS Policy & Exceptions
A GP refers a 45-year-old male to your pharmacy for counselling. He has been prescribed regular liquid paraffin as a laxative for his chronic constipation secondary to opioid analgesics for chronic non-cancer pain. His wife comes to collect the prescription and asks why the GP prescribed this rather than 'the fibre sachets from the shelf.' She mentions that constipation is listed on the NHS 35 conditions where prescribing should be avoided.
Which of the following BEST explains why prescribing is clinically justified in this case, consistent with NHS 2018 guidance?

Liquid paraffin is GSL/P — B is factually wrong. C misrepresents the guidance — the exception covers ongoing management needs, not exclusions by aetiology.

Topic: Medicine Classification & Reclassification

Case 4 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★☆ Advanced
Topic: Sunburn & Photodermatoses in Pregnancy
A 31-year-old female with confirmed polymorphic light eruption (PLE), 14 weeks pregnant, presents with mild sunburn (erythema, no blistering) on her arms after a beach trip. She asks about (i) analgesia for her sunburn pain and (ii) whether she qualifies for NHS-prescribed sunscreen and what specification to request.

KEY POINT: In pregnancy → paracetamol only (NSAIDs CI from 2nd trimester — premature ductus arteriosus closure risk). PLE is explicitly ACBS-approved for NHS sunscreen: minimum SPF 30+ broad-spectrum UVA+UVB. Year-round daily use required. Seasonal restriction is a common misconception. Independent prescriber pharmacists can prescribe ACBS items.
Case 5 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★☆ Advanced
Topic: Mild Acne — Benzoyl Peroxide Pharmacology
A 19-year-old male presents requesting 'the strongest acne product available.' He has mild-to-moderate comedonal and papulopustular acne. Six weeks of salicylic acid 2% wash produced minimal response. He takes no regular medications and mentions his girlfriend used 'the bleaching gel' effectively.
Which pharmacist recommendation regarding benzoyl peroxide (BPO) initiation BEST reflects the evidence base?
KEY POINT: BPO 2.5% = 10% antibacterially (equivalent efficacy). Start low, escalate for tolerability — optimises long-term adherence. 'Strongest first' rationale is not evidence-based.

Case 6 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★★ Expert
Topic: Laxative Pharmacology — Opioid-Induced Constipation
A 78-year-old female nursing home resident receives lactulose 30ml BD for 6 weeks for persistent severe constipation. She continues to strain daily. Medications: oxycodone 10mg SR BD, amlodipine 5mg OD, omeprazole 20mg OD. eGFR 48 ml/min.
Which laxative adjustment is MOST appropriate, with the strongest pharmacological rationale?
KEY POINT: Macrogol: non-fermented osmotic — no gas/bloating. Not systemically absorbed, not renally cleared → safe with eGFR 48. Superior to lactulose in elderly and opioid-induced constipation. Stimulant laxatives: max ~1 week (colonic atony risk). Bulk-forming agents require adequate fluid and peristalsis — ineffective in opioid hypomotility. Naloxegol = POM-only.
Case 7 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★★ Expert
Topic: Antibiotic-Associated Diarrhoea & C. difficile Risk
A 67-year-old male, 10 days post-hip replacement, presents with profuse watery diarrhoea (8–10 episodes/day) starting 3 days ago, low-grade fever (37.8°C), and abdominal cramping. He completed co-amoxiclav 625mg TDS for 7 days (post-operative prophylaxis) 5 days ago. He requests loperamide to control symptoms before an upcoming family event.
What is the MOST appropriate pharmacist intervention?
KEY POINT: Post-antibiotic + profuse diarrhoea + fever + elderly + post-surgical = C. difficile until proven otherwise. Loperamide ABSOLUTELY contraindicated — prevents toxin clearance → toxic megacolon risk. Treatment is fidaxomicin (preferred) or vancomycin. Probiotics have evidence for CDI prevention, NOT acute treatment. ORS alone without referral is insufficient.
Case 8 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★☆ Advanced
Topic: Allergic Rhinitis — Treatment Optimisation
A 29-year-old with moderate persistent house dust mite allergic rhinitis began fluticasone propionate 50mcg per nostril BD four days ago. She returns saying 'it's not working.' She also has severely itchy, watery eyes. Her only other medication is the combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP).
Which response to her consultation is MOST clinically accurate?
KEY POINT: Intranasal corticosteroids: 2–4 weeks for full effect — must be used REGULARLY, not PRN. Most common cause of apparent failure = PRN use + incorrect technique. Technique check is mandatory. Ketotifen or cromoglicate eye drops for ocular symptoms. No clinically significant pharmacokinetic interaction between nasal fluticasone (minimal systemic absorption) and COCPs.
Case 9 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★★ Expert
Topic: PPI Pharmacology, Drug Interactions & OTC Duration
A 55-year-old male has been purchasing OTC omeprazole 10mg daily for 9 months. He takes clopidogrel 75mg daily (post-coronary stent) and amlodipine 5mg. Symptoms recur before 2 weeks in each course. He asks you to 'make it stronger.'
Which combination of clinical concerns is MOST comprehensively and accurately identified?
KEY POINT: Omeprazole + clopidogrel = CYP2C19 inhibition → reduced clopidogrel activation → reduced antiplatelet effect → stent thrombosis risk (potentially life-threatening). Prefer pantoprazole or rabeprazole (lower CYP2C19 inhibition). OTC PPI >2 weeks without review = clinically inappropriate — persistent/recurrent symptoms in a 55-year-old need investigation.
Case 10 of 10 · 1 Mark★★★★★ Expert
Topic: Paracetamol Toxicology & Hidden Accumulation
A 34-year-old male with chronic low back pain and 15-year history of heavy alcohol use (>14 units/day) is purchasing: paracetamol 1g (32-tab pack), Night Nurse liquid (paracetamol 1g per 20ml dose), and asks to also take co-codamol 8/500 at night. Only other medication: diazepam 5mg PRN. He states he plans to take everything at maximum doses.
Which clinical risk assessment MOST accurately and comprehensively characterises this consultation?
KEY POINT: Chronic alcohol: CYP2E1 induction → more NAPQI (hepatotoxic metabolite) + glutathione depletion → hepatotoxicity at STANDARD therapeutic doses. Safe maximum = 2g/24h (or avoid entirely). Night Nurse = paracetamol + promethazine (sedating antihistamine) → additive CNS depression with diazepam. Hidden accumulation: always counsel on ALL paracetamol-containing products.

2Create the Apps Script
  1. In your Google Sheet, click Extensions → Apps Script
  2. Delete any existing code and paste the following:
function doPost(e) {
  try {
    var sheet = SpreadsheetApp.getActiveSpreadsheet().getActiveSheet();
    var data = JSON.parse(e.postData.contents);
    var answers = data.answers; // {Q1:"A", Q2:"C", ...}
    var correct = {Q1:"A",Q2:"C",Q3:"B",Q4:"C",Q5:"B",
                   Q6:"B",Q7:"C",Q8:"C",Q9:"A",Q10:"C"};
    
    var correctCount = 0;
    var qCorrectArr = [];
    for (var i = 1; i <= 10; i++) {
      var key = "Q" + i;
      var isRight = (answers[key] === correct[key]) ? 1 : 0;
      correctCount += isRight;
      qCorrectArr.push(isRight ? "✓" : "✗ (" + correct[key] + ")");
    }
    var marks = correctCount * 1;
    var pct = Math.round((marks / 10) * 100);
    
    var row = [
      new Date(),
      data.studentId,
      data.studentName,
      data.section || "",
      data.email || "",
      answers.Q1||"", answers.Q2||"", answers.Q3||"",
      answers.Q4||"", answers.Q5||"", answers.Q6||"",
      answers.Q7||"", answers.Q8||"", answers.Q9||"",
      answers.Q10||"",
      correctCount,
      marks,
      pct + "%"
    ].concat(qCorrectArr);
    
    sheet.appendRow(row);
    
    return ContentService
      .createTextOutput(JSON.stringify({
        success: true,
        marks: marks,
        correct: correctCount,
        percentage: pct
      }))
      .setMimeType(ContentService.MimeType.JSON);
  } catch(err) {
    return ContentService
      .createTextOutput(JSON.stringify({success:false, error:err.toString()}))
      .setMimeType(ContentService.MimeType.JSON);
  }
}

function doGet(e) {
  return ContentService
    .createTextOutput("NHS Self-Care Module - Submission endpoint active")
    .setMimeType(ContentService.MimeType.TEXT);
}
